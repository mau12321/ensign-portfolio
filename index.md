Ethical Dilemma Analysis: Data Sovereignty and Indigenous Privacy
In the development of the Visit Peru Now platform, I face a critical ethical dilemma regarding Technological Expansion versus Indigenous Data Privacy. To successfully "impulse" tourism, the platform must collect precise GPS coordinates of remote villages and personal financial records of traditional artisans (e.g., Inka knitters). The dilemma lies in the fact that these vulnerable populations often lack digital literacy and may not fully understand the implications of their digital footprints being stored on a cloud server.
To analyze this, I looked at the catastrophic 2018 Marriott Starwood breach. During a corporate acquisition, Marriott's leadership prioritized rapid growth and integration over rigorous security audits, resulting in the exposure of 339 million guests' travel patterns and passport data (Fruhlinger, 2020). The ethical failure was not just the technical vulnerability; it was the violation of professional duties to minimize harm and protect public safety. Their delayed response and lack of field-level encryption directly breached the trust of their users.
Learning from this utilitarian failure, I am applying the ACM Code of Ethics and the spiritual principle of integrity. I refuse to prioritize rapid user acquisition over the safety of the villages I serve. To resolve this dilemma ethically, I am implementing "Privacy by Design" and robust Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) in the Lamanita app. Sensitive village locations and financial data are heavily encrypted and only revealed to thoroughly verified travelers. By doing so, I ensure that my software empowers these communities with economic liberty without exploiting their data or exposing them to modern digital threats.
